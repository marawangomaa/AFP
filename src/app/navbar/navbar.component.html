Introduction

Gastrodiplomacy is how nations wield cuisine to build influence, from embassy dinners where friendship with the host notion and a positive image are sealed over shared meals to viral Twitter wars over whose Jollof reigns supreme. The Jollof Rice debate is a long-standing one

The Jollof rice recipe is extremely diverse, as each West African country has its own variation, and the dish is very flexible to the point that anyone can add their personal touch to it. In every variety of the region, the primary components are still tomatoes, pepper, oil, and spices.

In this section, we will talk about two variations.

1-Ghana Jollof Variation

The particularity about Ghana Jollof is its colorfulness, flavor, texture, and aroma. Ghanaians tend to make their Jollof foundation stew less liquid and prepare the Jollof with heat instead of a lot of water because they dislike their rice being overly soft. So Ghana jollof is neither too hard or soft. Ghana Jollof is typically served with coleslaw or a salad, paired with the well-known black chili sauce (called “shito”) or the green variation (called “Kpapishito”) that accompanied every Ghanaian’s food. Pairing it with fried plantain in not uncommon It goes well with chicken, beef, goat, sausage, gizzards or tilapia fish whether fried or grilled. However, in the Ghanaian Assorted form of Jollof rice, some of the various proteins specified are diced and added to the Jollof rice pot five minutes before it is turned off the heat, along with chopped vegetables (green pepper, carrot, spring onion, etc.). 2-Benin Jollof Variation

Jollof rice is popularly known as “Riz au gras” in Benin. The difference with the Ghanaian variation is the fact that the stew base is a little more watery.

It is typically consumed with “Jus,” a chopped onion stew or a combination of chopped onion and tomato stew. For people who can handle a lot of spiciness, they occasionally add ground seasoned pepper. Beninese people have preferences fried fish, turkey, and traditional cheeses called “Wagashi” or “Fromage Peulh,” but “Riz au gras” can be served with any protein.

During special occasions or celebrations, it can be accompanied with a potato salad known as “Hors d’oeuvre.”
